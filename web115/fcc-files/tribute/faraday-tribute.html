Michael Faraday

Michael Faraday (FRS) was a english physicist who was instrumental to our modern understanding of electromagnetism. Faraday was so important to our understanding of this fundemental force of the universe that Albert Einstein kept a picture of him on his study wall, along with those of Isaac Newton and James Clerk Maxwell.

  • 1791: Michael Faraday is born into a poor family in London.
  • 1812: Faraday successfully performs electrolysis on sulfate of magnesium, using a voltic pile made out of seven brittish halfpennies, sheets of zinc, and paper soaked in salt-water. This is well before the process of electrolysis is well-understood.
  • 1821: Faraday invents the earliest form of electric motor, built out of a magnet, a pool of mercury, a wire, and a chemical battery. This is the first experiment to prove that electric currents and magnets can interact to produce controled motion.
  • 1831: Faraday builds the world's first electric generator, centred around a horseshoe-shaped magnet and a disk of copper spinning between its poles.
  • 1832: Faraday successfully conducts a series of experiments proving that electricity from various sources (static, battery-fed, bioelectric) all behaves the same way, refuting the then-popular idea that these sources all produce separate phenomena.
  • 1836: Faraday tests an apparatus now know as a Faraday-cage, entering a small room with charged wires running along all walls, and emerging unharmed by electricity. This proves his theory that electricity repels itself.
  • 1845: Faraday conducts the first experiments showing that light bends around magnets, showing some of the first experimental proof of a link between light and electromagnetism, and also setting the stage for the modern visualization of magnetic fields.
  • 1867: Michael Faraday dies at the age of 75.
